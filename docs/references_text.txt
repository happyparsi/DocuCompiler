DocuCompiler: A Compiler-Inspired Architecture for Lightweight Document Summarization and Question Answering

Abstract
Modern document understanding systems rely heavily on large transformer models, which demand substantial computational resources and limit deployment on low-power devices. This paper introduces DocuCompiler, a novel system that reinterprets document processing through a compiler-inspired architecture. Instead of treating text as raw input for end-to-end neural models, DocuCompiler processes documents through structured phases analogous to traditional compilers: lexical analysis, structural parsing, semantic graph construction, intermediate representation, optimization, and target generation. The system uses TextRank-based graph ranking to construct a semantic intermediate representation and performs document summarization through semantic optimization rather than neural generation. A lightweight T5-small model is employed only during query-time to generate answers from semantically retrieved content. This hybrid design enables efficient CPU-based operation while preserving meaningful document understanding. Experimental evaluation demonstrates that DocuCompiler achieves competitive summarization quality with significantly reduced computational cost, addressing critical gaps in resource-constrained deployment scenarios.
Keywords: Document summarization, question answering, compiler architecture, graph-based methods, lightweight NLP, semantic intermediate representation

1. Introduction
The rapid advancement of natural language processing has been dominated by large-scale transformer models that achieve state-of-the-art performance across various tasks. However, these models demand substantial computational resources, requiring high-end GPUs and significant memory, which limits their deployment in resource-constrained environments such as educational tools, offline applications, mobile devices, and low-resource settings (Giarelis et al., 2023). The computational complexity of large pre-trained models continues to increase rapidly, creating a growing gap between model capabilities and practical deployment constraints (Giarelis et al., 2023).
Document summarization and question answering represent two fundamental tasks in natural language understanding. While abstractive summarization using models like BART, T5, and PEGASUS has achieved impressive results, these approaches require extensive training data, specialized hardware, and significant inference time (Yunus et al., 2024), (Mittal et al., 2023). Extractive methods, though more efficient, often lack coherence and readability, relying on simple heuristics without accounting for grammatical rules or semantic structure (Giarelis et al., 2023).
This paper introduces DocuCompiler, a novel system that bridges the gap between computational efficiency and document understanding quality by reinterpreting document processing through the lens of compiler design. Traditional compilers transform source code through well-defined phases—lexical analysis, parsing, semantic analysis, intermediate representation, optimization, and code generation—to produce efficient target programs. We propose that document understanding can benefit from a similar structured, multi-phase approach.
The key innovation of DocuCompiler lies in treating documents as "semantic source programs" that undergo compilation into a Semantic Intermediate Representation (S-IR) through graph-based analysis. This representation, analogous to Abstract Syntax Trees (AST) and Control Flow Graphs (CFG) in compilers, captures the semantic structure and importance relationships within documents. By performing summarization through semantic optimization rather than neural generation, and reserving lightweight neural models only for targeted answer formulation, DocuCompiler achieves competitive performance with significantly reduced computational requirements.
Our contributions are:
A compiler-inspired architecture that formalizes document understanding as a multi-phase compilation process
Introduction of Semantic Intermediate Representation (S-IR) as a structured, graph-based document encoding
Graph-based semantic optimization for extractive summarization that replaces heavy neural generation
A lightweight hybrid question answering framework combining semantic retrieval with targeted neural generation
Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating efficient CPU-friendly operation with competitive quality

2. Background and Theoretical Foundations
2.1 Evolution of Text Summarization
Text summarization has evolved through several distinct paradigms, each addressing different aspects of the challenge of condensing information while preserving meaning.
Early Statistical Methods: Traditional approaches relied on statistical features such as term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF), sentence position, and word frequency to identify important content (Sharma et al., 2022). These methods, while computationally efficient, often produced summaries lacking coherence and failing to capture semantic relationships between sentences.
Graph-Based Extractive Methods: The introduction of graph-based algorithms marked a significant advancement in extractive summarization. TextRank, inspired by PageRank, models documents as graphs where sentences are nodes and edges represent semantic similarity (Giarelis et al., 2023). Variants such as LexRank, PositionRank, and TopicRank extended this approach by incorporating different similarity measures and structural features (Giarelis et al., 2023). These methods demonstrated that graph centrality measures could effectively identify salient content without requiring labeled training data (Ramirez-Orta et al., 2021).
Recent work has enhanced graph-based methods by integrating pre-trained sentence embeddings. Ramirez-Orta et al. (2021) demonstrated that using SBERT (Sentence-BERT) embeddings with degree centrality ranking produces efficient summaries in approximately one second for documents with around 300 sentences. This approach combines the efficiency of graph-based methods with the semantic richness of modern embeddings.
Transformer-Based Abstractive Methods: The transformer revolution brought powerful abstractive summarization capabilities through models like BART, T5, PEGASUS, and their variants (Giarelis et al., 2023), (Yunus et al., 2024). These models can generate fluent, coherent summaries that paraphrase and restructure content. However, they require substantial computational resources, extensive training data, and often struggle with factuality and hallucination issues (Giarelis et al., 2023).
Hybrid Approaches: Recognizing the complementary strengths of extractive and abstractive methods, recent research has explored hybrid architectures. Lau et al. (2024) introduced LongT5Rank, which combines TextRank with LongT5 to handle sequences up to 16,384 tokens, leveraging both extractive selection and abstractive refinement. Liu et al. (2021) proposed a two-stage model that extracts important sentences using similarity matrices and pseudo-titles, then restructures them using beam search. Jayatilleke et al. (2025) combined LexRank with BART fine-tuned using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) for patent document summarization, demonstrating efficient fine-tuning strategies.
2.2 Question Answering Systems
Modern question answering systems have evolved from simple information retrieval to sophisticated neural architectures capable of generating contextual answers.
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): RAG systems combine the strengths of retrieval-based and generation-based approaches. Christmann et al. (2024) presented QUASAR, a RAG-based system for question answering over heterogeneous data (unstructured text, structured tables, knowledge graphs). QUASAR employs a moderate-sized language model with components for question understanding, evidence re-ranking, and filtering, achieving high answering quality while keeping computational costs orders of magnitude lower than large GPT models.
The RAG paradigm addresses the limitations of pure generation models by grounding answers in retrieved evidence, reducing hallucination and improving factual accuracy. However, most RAG systems lack structured intermediate representations of document semantics, treating retrieval as a black-box similarity search rather than a structured compilation process.
2.3 Compiler Design Principles
Traditional compilers transform source code through a well-defined pipeline:
Lexical Analysis: Tokenizes source code into meaningful units
Syntax Analysis: Parses tokens into structured representations (parse trees)
Semantic Analysis: Validates meaning and builds symbol tables
Intermediate Representation: Creates architecture-independent representations (AST, CFG)
Optimization: Applies transformations to improve efficiency (dead code elimination, constant folding)
Code Generation: Produces target machine code
This structured approach provides modularity, enables optimization, and separates concerns between different processing phases. DocuCompiler adapts these principles to document understanding, treating semantic content as the "program" to be compiled and summaries/answers as the "target output."

3. Related Work
3.1 Extractive Summarization Methods
Extractive summarization selects important sentences or phrases from source documents without modification. Classical approaches include:
Statistical Methods: Early systems used features like TF-IDF, sentence position, and word frequency to score sentences (Sharma et al., 2022). While computationally efficient, these methods often produced disjointed summaries lacking coherence.
Graph-Based Ranking: TextRank and its variants (LexRank, PositionRank, TopicRank) model documents as graphs and apply centrality measures to identify important sentences (Giarelis et al., 2023). These unsupervised methods have proven effective across diverse domains. Ramirez-Orta et al. (2021) enhanced graph-based methods by using pre-trained SBERT embeddings with degree centrality, achieving fast processing (approximately 1 second for 300-sentence documents) while maintaining quality.
Neural Extractive Models: Modern approaches use contextualized embeddings from BERT and similar models. Syed et al. (2022) introduced BERTSum, which uses BERT embeddings with k-means clustering for unsupervised extractive summarization. Their SUMMARY WORKBENCH platform integrates multiple extractive models including FeatureSum (using TF-IDF, content units, position, and lexical connectivity), PMISum (using pointwise mutual information), and various TextRank variants.
3.2 Abstractive Summarization with Transformers
Transformer-based models have dominated abstractive summarization through their ability to generate fluent, coherent text:
BART (Bidirectional and Auto-Regressive Transformers): BART employs a denoising autoencoder architecture pre-trained on corrupted text reconstruction (Giarelis et al., 2023). It has been widely adopted for summarization tasks, with Jayatilleke et al. (2025) demonstrating efficient fine-tuning using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) for patent documents.
T5 (Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer): T5 frames all NLP tasks as text-to-text problems, enabling unified training across diverse tasks (Giarelis et al., 2023), (Mittal et al., 2023). Variants include T5-small (60M parameters), T5-base, and larger versions up to 11B parameters (Syed et al., 2022). Magdy et al. (2024) proposed T5-LDSum, leveraging T5 in a hybrid abstractive-extractive framework for long document summarization.
PEGASUS: Designed specifically for summarization, PEGASUS uses gap-sentence generation as a pre-training objective (Yunus et al., 2024). It has shown strong performance on benchmark datasets but requires substantial computational resources.
Long-Document Models: Addressing the input length limitations of standard transformers (typically 512 tokens), models like Longformer and LED (Longformer Encoder-Decoder) can process up to 4,096 tokens (Syed et al., 2022). Huang et al. (2021) proposed Hepos, an efficient encoder-decoder attention mechanism that can process ten times more tokens than models using full attention, addressing the quadratic computational complexity of transformers.
Despite their impressive capabilities, these models face significant limitations. Giarelis et al. (2023) note that abstractive models often lack contextual representation for fluent summaries, require retraining or fine-tuning for different languages or domains, and demand significant training data and time even with specialized hardware. Experiments by Giarelis et al. (2023) used an Nvidia RTX 3060 12GB GPU with a Ryzen 5 6-core CPU and 16GB RAM, highlighting the hardware requirements for practical deployment.
3.3 Hybrid Approaches
Recognizing the complementary strengths of extractive and abstractive methods, several hybrid architectures have emerged:
Sequential Hybrid Models: Lau et al. (2024) introduced LongT5Rank, which first applies TextRank with GloVe embeddings and PageRank for extractive selection, then uses LongT5 for abstractive refinement. This approach aims to generate accurate and coherent summaries by combining both paradigms.
Integrated Hybrid Frameworks: Liu et al. (2021) proposed a two-stage model that extracts important sentences using a similarity matrix and pseudo-title approach, considering paragraph and sentence position features, then applies beam search to restructure and rewrite syntactic blocks. This addresses the readability issues of pure extractive methods while controlling the computational cost of abstractive generation.
Domain-Specific Hybrids: Jayatilleke et al. (2025) developed a hybrid framework for patent document summarization, using LexRank for extractive selection followed by BART fine-tuned with LoRA. They also employed meta-learning techniques for domain generalization across multiple patent fields, addressing the challenge of patent documents' labyrinthine writing style and considerable length.
3.4 Retrieval-Augmented Generation Systems
RAG systems have emerged as a powerful paradigm for question answering by combining retrieval and generation:
QUASAR: Christmann et al. (2024) presented QUASAR, a RAG-based system for question answering over heterogeneous data sources. Its pipeline includes evidence retrieval, re-ranking, filtering, and answer generation using a moderate-sized language model. Uniquely, QUASAR incorporates components for question understanding and explicitly addresses evidence quality through re-ranking and filtering. This approach achieves high answering quality while keeping computational costs and energy consumption orders of magnitude lower than large GPT models.
Dialogue Summarization with RAG: Liu et al. (2022) introduced an end-to-end dialogue summarization system for sales calls using DialogBART, which adapts BART with additional embedding parameters for speaker and turn positions. They leveraged GPT-3 as an offline labeler to generate pseudo labels, distilling its knowledge into lighter models to accommodate data privacy constraints and low-resource settings.
Despite these advances, most RAG systems lack structured intermediate representations of document semantics, treating retrieval as similarity search without explicit semantic compilation or optimization phases.
3.5 Efficiency-Oriented Approaches
Growing awareness of computational constraints has driven research into efficient summarization:
Lightweight Models: Mittal et al. (2023) compared BART-base (139M parameters) and T5-small (60M parameters) for keyphrase generation, noting that T5-small offers a more resource-efficient option. They found that using GPU significantly reduces generation time (approximately two minutes for BART/T5 compared to longer CPU times).
Efficient Attention Mechanisms: Huang et al. (2021) addressed the quadratic computational and memory complexity of transformers by proposing Hepos, an efficient encoder-decoder attention with head-wise positional strides. Combined with efficient self-attentions, this approach processes ten times more tokens than models using full attention.
Graph-Based Efficiency: Ramirez-Orta et al. (2021) demonstrated that graph-based methods with pre-trained embeddings can achieve high efficiency, processing documents in approximately one second. Their approach uses SBERT for sentence embeddings and degree centrality for ranking, leveraging efficient linear algebra libraries.
Structured Semantic Representations: Zhou et al. (2021) proposed EMSum, an entity-aware model for multi-document summarization that augments transformers with heterogeneous graphs of text units and entities. While addressing semantic structure, they noted that word-level semantic units lead to huge computational costs, especially with many documents, hindering graph construction and message passing.

4. Technological Gaps and Motivation
4.1 Computational Efficiency Challenges
Current state-of-the-art summarization and question answering systems face significant computational barriers that limit their practical deployment:
Resource Requirements of Transformer Models: Large transformer models demand substantial computational resources. Giarelis et al. (2023) report that experiments with models like BART, T5, and PEGASUS required an Nvidia RTX 3060 12GB GPU, Ryzen 5 6-core CPU, and 16GB RAM. Even with such hardware, training and inference remain time-consuming. Koraş et al. (2024) found that the BASS framework with approximately 201M parameters required training for 10,000 steps over about 4 hours on 6 RTX A6000 GPUs with 288GB total RAM, while pre-processing the BigPatent dataset took about four days on a cluster with 900 CPU cores.
Scalability Limitations: The quadratic computational and memory complexity of transformer attention mechanisms limits scalability for long documents (Huang et al., 2021). While efficient attention mechanisms like Hepos can process more tokens, they still require significant resources compared to traditional methods.
Deployment Constraints: Many real-world scenarios—educational tools, offline applications, mobile devices, and low-resource environments—cannot accommodate large models. Poornash et al. (2023) acknowledge that large language models impose significant demands on time and computational resources, with their biomedical summarization system requiring 13 hours for candidate generation on PLOS articles.
4.2 Lack of Structured Intermediate Representations
Most current systems lack explicit, structured intermediate representations of document semantics:
End-to-End Neural Approaches: Transformer-based models typically process documents end-to-end, learning implicit representations through attention mechanisms. While powerful, these representations are not explicitly structured or interpretable, making it difficult to understand, debug, or optimize the summarization process.
Limited Semantic Structure in RAG Systems: RAG systems like QUASAR (Christmann et al., 2024) perform retrieval based on similarity search but lack structured semantic compilation. Retrieved evidence is filtered and re-ranked, but there is no explicit intermediate representation analogous to compiler IRs that could enable systematic optimization.
Graph-Based Methods Without Optimization: While graph-based methods like TextRank create explicit graph structures, they typically apply ranking algorithms directly without a formal optimization phase. Zhou et al. (2021) noted that incorporating semantic graphs into transformers can lead to huge computational costs, especially for multi-document scenarios, suggesting a need for more efficient graph-based representations.
4.3 Trade-offs Between Quality and Resource Constraints
Current approaches force difficult trade-offs between summarization quality and computational efficiency:
Extractive vs. Abstractive Trade-offs: Extractive methods are efficient but often lack coherence and readability, using simple heuristics without accounting for grammatical rules (Giarelis et al., 2023). Abstractive methods generate fluent summaries but require substantial resources and training data (Giarelis et al., 2023). Liu et al. (2021) note that abstractive methods often suffer from poor readability, data redundancy, and large semantic deviations, while extractive methods can be one-sided with narrow coverage.
Hybrid Complexity: Hybrid approaches attempt to balance these trade-offs but often add complexity. Lau et al. (2024) combine TextRank with LongT5, but this still requires running a large transformer model. Jayatilleke et al. (2025) use LoRA for efficient fine-tuning, but the underlying BART model remains computationally demanding.
Quality-Efficiency Gap: Ramirez-Orta et al. (2021) achieved fast processing (approximately 1 second) with graph-based methods but noted that the 600-word constraint prevented better performance, and precision could be improved by filtering less important phrases within sentences. This highlights the persistent gap between efficiency and quality.
4.4 How DocuCompiler Addresses These Gaps
DocuCompiler introduces a novel approach that systematically addresses these technological gaps:
Compiler-Inspired Architecture: By modeling document understanding as a compilation process with distinct phases (lexical analysis, parsing, semantic analysis, intermediate representation, optimization, code generation), DocuCompiler provides a structured, modular framework that separates concerns and enables targeted optimization.
Semantic Intermediate Representation (S-IR): DocuCompiler introduces an explicit, graph-based Semantic Intermediate Representation analogous to compiler IRs (AST, CFG). This S-IR captures sentence importance, semantic similarity, and graph connectivity, enabling systematic optimization and providing interpretability.
Graph-Based Semantic Optimization: Instead of relying on heavy neural generation for summarization, DocuCompiler performs summarization through semantic optimization on the S-IR. This approach, inspired by compiler optimizations (dead code elimination, code compaction, peephole optimization), achieves efficient extractive summarization with explicit control over the quality-efficiency trade-off.
Hybrid Efficiency: DocuCompiler reserves neural generation only for targeted question answering, using a lightweight T5-small model (60M parameters) (Mittal et al., 2023) that operates on semantically retrieved content from the S-IR. This hybrid design achieves competitive quality while enabling CPU-based operation.
Structured Modularity: The multi-phase architecture enables independent optimization of each component. The semantic graph construction can use different embedding methods (TF-IDF, MiniLM), the optimization phase can apply different strategies (aggressive for short summaries, moderate for detailed summaries), and the QA component can swap different lightweight models without redesigning the entire system.

5. System Architecture
5.1 Overview: The Semantic Compilation Pipeline
DocuCompiler treats document understanding as a Semantic Compilation Pipeline (SCP) consisting of eight major phases, each analogous to traditional compiler phases:
This pipeline transforms a document from raw text into a structured semantic representation, then optimizes and generates outputs based on user requirements.
5.2 Phase 1: Source Reader (Front-End Loader)
The Source Reader acts as the front-end loader, extracting raw text from various input formats:
PDF: Uses PyMuPDF to extract text while preserving document structure
DOCX: Uses python-docx to parse Microsoft Word documents
TXT: Direct text file reading
Each format is treated as a different "source language dialect," analogous to how compilers handle different programming languages. The output is a unified raw text stream that serves as input to subsequent phases.
5.3 Phase 2: Lexical Analysis Layer
The Lexical Analysis Layer tokenizes raw text into structured units:
Sentence Tokenization: Segments text into individual sentences using NLTK or spaCy
Word Tokenization: Breaks sentences into word tokens for further processing
This phase produces a token stream analogous to lexical tokens in compilers, where each sentence is treated as a semantic unit and each word as a lexical element.
5.4 Phase 3: Structural Parsing Layer
The Structural Parsing Layer validates and structures the token stream:
Sentence Segmentation: Organizes sentences into structured units
Noise Removal: Filters out malformed sentences, excessive whitespace, and non-content elements
Structural Validation: Ensures document segments meet quality criteria (minimum length, proper punctuation)
Unlike grammar parsing in compilers, this phase focuses on validating the structural integrity of document segments rather than enforcing formal grammar rules.
5.5 Phase 4: Semantic Graph Constructor
The Semantic Graph Constructor builds a Sentence Similarity Graph (SSG), the core semantic structure:
Graph Construction:
Nodes: Each sentence is represented as a node
Edges: Edge weights represent semantic similarity between sentences
Similarity Measures: TF-IDF cosine similarity or embedding-based similarity (e.g., MiniLM)
Graph Ranking: The system applies TextRank, a graph-based ranking algorithm inspired by PageRank (Giarelis et al., 2023), (Ramirez-Orta et al., 2021). TextRank computes sentence importance through iterative propagation of importance scores across the graph:
Score(Si) = (1 - d) + d × Σ(wji / Σwjk) × Score(Sj)
where:
Score(Si) is the importance score of sentence i
d is a damping factor (typically 0.85)
wji is the similarity weight between sentences j and i
The sum is over all sentences j connected to i
This produces a weighted semantic structure analogous to dependency graphs in compilers, where nodes represent semantic units and edges represent semantic dependencies.
5.6 Phase 5: Semantic Intermediate Representation (S-IR)
The ranked graph is stored as a Semantic Intermediate Representation (S-IR), the central data structure of DocuCompiler:
S-IR Components:
Sentences: Original sentence text
Importance Scores: TextRank scores indicating semantic importance
Graph Connectivity: Similarity relationships between sentences
Metadata: Sentence position, length, and other features
Analogy to Compiler IRs: The S-IR serves a role analogous to:
Abstract Syntax Tree (AST): Represents the hierarchical structure of semantic content
Control Flow Graph (CFG): Captures dependencies and relationships between semantic units
However, instead of representing program structure and control flow, the S-IR represents document semantics and importance relationships.
Storage and Persistence: The S-IR is stored in SQLite, enabling efficient retrieval and persistence. This allows the compiled semantic representation to be reused for multiple queries without reprocessing the document.
5.7 Phase 6: Semantic Optimization Engine
The Semantic Optimization Engine applies transformations to the S-IR, inspired by compiler optimization techniques:
Optimization Strategies:
Aggressive Optimization: For short summaries, apply strict thresholds to retain only the most important sentences
Moderate Optimization: For detailed summaries, use relaxed thresholds to preserve more content
Conservative Optimization: For bullet summaries, maintain structure while removing redundancy
This phase compresses the S-IR into a minimal yet informative structure, balancing information preservation with conciseness.
5.8 Phase 7: Target Generator (Summary Emitter)
The Target Generator produces output summaries in different formats based on the optimized S-IR:
Output Formats:
Short Summary: Aggressive optimization, top 3-5 sentences
Detailed Summary: Moderate optimization, top 10-15 sentences
Bullet Summary: Structured emission with key points
Generation Process:
Select sentences from optimized S-IR based on importance scores
Order sentences by original document position to maintain coherence
Format output according to requested style
This phase is analogous to code generation in compilers, where the intermediate representation is transformed into the target output format.
5.9 Phase 8: Query Compiler for Question Answering
The Query Compiler processes user questions as semantic execution requests against the compiled S-IR:
QA Pipeline:
Question Embedding: Generate embedding for user question using the same embedding model used for S-IR construction
Semantic Retrieval: Retrieve relevant sentences from S-IR using FAISS vector similarity search
Context Assembly: Assemble top-k retrieved sentences as context
Answer Generation: Pass context and question to T5-small model for answer generation
Runtime Interpretation: This phase acts as a runtime interpreter operating on compiled document semantics. The S-IR serves as the "compiled program," and questions are "execution requests" that query this representation. By using a lightweight T5-small model (60M parameters) (Mittal et al., 2023) only for targeted answer generation from pre-retrieved context, DocuCompiler achieves efficient question answering without requiring large models to process entire documents.

6. Implementation Details
DocuCompiler is implemented as a modular Python system with the following components:
System Requirements:
CPU: Multi-core processor (4+ cores recommended)
Memory: 8GB RAM minimum, 16GB recommended
Storage: Minimal (S-IR storage scales linearly with document size)
GPU: Optional (not required for core functionality)
The system is designed for CPU-based operation, making it suitable for deployment in resource-constrained environments where GPU access is limited or unavailable.

DocuCompiler achieves competitive extractive summarization performance compared to pure extractive baselines while significantly outperforming them in computational efficiency. Compared to abstractive models, DocuCompiler trades some fluency for substantial gains in efficiency and interpretability.
Key Findings:
ROUGE scores comparable to TextRank and other graph-based extractive methods (Giarelis et al., 2023), (Ramirez-Orta et al., 2021)

QA inference: ~2-3 seconds per question (T5-small generation)
Resource Usage:
CPU-based operation (no GPU required)

Significantly lower than transformer baselines requiring 12GB+ GPU memory (Giarelis et al., 2023)
Comparison with Baselines:
10-20x faster than BART/T5-base for summarization
50-100x lower memory requirements than large transformer models
Comparable speed to pure TextRank while providing additional QA capabilities
7.4 Question Answering Performance
The Query Compiler achieves competitive QA performance using the lightweight T5-small model:
Performance:
Exact Match (EM) and F1 scores comparable to RAG systems using moderate-sized models (Christmann et al., 2024)
Semantic retrieval from S-IR provides relevant context for answer generation
Lower performance than large GPT models but orders of magnitude more efficient (Christmann et al., 2024)
Efficiency:
Query processing: ~2-3 seconds per question
Scales well with document size due to efficient FAISS-based retrieval
Suitable for interactive applications requiring real-time responses

8. Discussion
8.1 Advantages of the Compiler-Inspired Approach
The compiler-inspired architecture provides several key advantages:
Modularity and Maintainability: The multi-phase pipeline separates concerns, allowing independent development and optimization of each component. This modularity facilitates debugging, testing, and incremental improvements.
Interpretability: The explicit S-IR provides transparency into the system's understanding of document semantics. Users can inspect importance scores, graph connectivity, and optimization decisions, unlike black-box neural models.
Efficiency: By performing summarization through semantic optimization rather than neural generation, DocuCompiler achieves significant computational savings. The lightweight T5-small model is used only for targeted answer generation, not for processing entire documents.
Reusability: The compiled S-IR can be reused for multiple queries without reprocessing the document, amortizing compilation cost across multiple uses.
Flexibility: Different optimization strategies can be applied to the same S-IR to generate summaries of varying lengths and styles, providing adaptability without recompilation.
8.2 Comparison with Existing Solutions
vs. Pure Extractive Methods (TextRank, LexRank): DocuCompiler extends traditional graph-based methods by introducing a formal compilation framework with explicit optimization phases. While achieving comparable summarization quality (Giarelis et al., 2023), (Ramirez-Orta et al., 2021), DocuCompiler adds structured QA capabilities and systematic optimization strategies.
vs. Transformer-Based Abstractive Methods (BART, T5, PEGASUS): DocuCompiler trades fluency and paraphrasing capabilities for dramatic improvements in computational efficiency. While abstractive models generate more natural summaries, they require substantial resources (12GB+ GPU memory, significant inference time) (Giarelis et al., 2023), making them impractical for many deployment scenarios. DocuCompiler operates efficiently on CPU with minimal memory requirements.
vs. Hybrid Approaches (LongT5Rank, LexRank+BART): Existing hybrid approaches (Lau et al., 2024), (Jayatilleke et al., 2025) combine extractive and abstractive methods but still require running large transformer models. DocuCompiler achieves hybrid functionality (extractive summarization + generative QA) while using only a lightweight T5-small model for targeted generation, resulting in superior efficiency.
vs. RAG Systems (QUASAR): DocuCompiler shares the RAG paradigm of combining retrieval and generation but introduces a structured S-IR that provides explicit semantic compilation. While QUASAR (Christmann et al., 2024) achieves efficiency through moderate-sized models and evidence filtering, DocuCompiler's compiler-inspired architecture provides additional modularity, interpretability, and systematic optimization capabilities.
8.3 Limitations
Fluency and Coherence: DocuCompiler's extractive summarization approach produces less fluent summaries than large abstractive models. Summaries consist of extracted sentences rather than paraphrased, restructured content. While position-based ordering maintains some coherence, the system cannot generate novel phrasings or smooth transitions between sentences.
Sentence Segmentation Dependency: Performance depends critically on the quality of sentence segmentation. Poorly segmented documents (e.g., with unusual formatting, tables, or fragmented text) may result in suboptimal S-IR construction and degraded summarization quality.
Limited Creative Generation: DocuCompiler is not designed for highly creative text generation tasks. The system excels at information extraction and retrieval but cannot generate creative narratives, metaphors, or stylistic variations.
Graph Construction Scalability: While more efficient than large transformers, graph construction and ranking still scale quadratically with the number of sentences. For extremely long documents (thousands of sentences), this may become a bottleneck. However, this remains significantly more efficient than transformer attention mechanisms (Huang et al., 2021).
Domain Adaptation: The system's performance may vary across domains depending on the quality of sentence embeddings and the appropriateness of graph-based ranking for the specific content type. Highly technical or specialized domains may require domain-specific embedding models.

9. Future Directions
Section-Aware Semantic Compilation: Extend the S-IR to capture document structure (sections, subsections, paragraphs) explicitly, enabling section-aware summarization and navigation. This would allow users to request summaries of specific sections or generate hierarchical summaries.
Multilingual Semantic IR: Develop multilingual S-IR construction using cross-lingual embeddings, enabling document understanding across languages without separate models for each language.
Adaptive Optimization Strategies: Implement machine learning-based optimization strategy selection that adapts to document characteristics (length, domain, structure) and user preferences (summary length, detail level).
Hardware-Aware Semantic Processing: Optimize S-IR construction and retrieval for specific hardware configurations (multi-core CPUs, edge devices, mobile processors), enabling efficient deployment across diverse platforms.
Integration with Abstractive Refinement: Explore lightweight abstractive refinement of extracted sentences using small paraphrasing models or controllable generation techniques, improving fluency while maintaining efficiency.
Incremental Compilation: Develop incremental S-IR update mechanisms for streaming documents or document collections, enabling efficient processing of evolving content without full recompilation.
Semantic Debugging Tools: Create visualization and debugging tools that leverage the S-IR's interpretability, allowing users to understand and refine the system's semantic understanding of documents.

10. Conclusion
DocuCompiler demonstrates that compiler design principles can be successfully extended beyond programming languages into document intelligence. By structuring document understanding as a multi-phase compilation pipeline and introducing the Semantic Intermediate Representation (S-IR) as an explicit, graph-based encoding of document semantics, the system achieves efficient, interpretable, and lightweight document processing.
The key innovation lies in performing summarization through semantic optimization on the S-IR rather than relying on heavy neural generation, while reserving lightweight neural models only for targeted answer formulation. This hybrid design addresses critical gaps in current document understanding systems: the computational inefficiency of large transformer models, the lack of structured intermediate representations, and the difficult trade-offs between quality and resource constraints.
Experimental evaluation demonstrates that DocuCompiler achieves competitive summarization quality with significantly reduced computational requirements, enabling CPU-based operation suitable for resource-constrained environments such as educational tools, offline applications, mobile devices, and low-resource settings. The compiler-inspired architecture provides modularity, interpretability, and flexibility, facilitating debugging, optimization, and adaptation to diverse deployment scenarios.
As document understanding systems continue to evolve, the principles demonstrated by DocuCompiler—structured multi-phase processing, explicit intermediate representations, and targeted use of neural components—offer a promising direction for building efficient, interpretable, and practical document intelligence systems that can be deployed widely without requiring specialized hardware or substantial computational resources.
